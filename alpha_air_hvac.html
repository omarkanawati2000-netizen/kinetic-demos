Common Questions

FAQ — Colorado HVAC Answers

Questions about heating and cooling in Colorado's unique climate? We've got answers.

How often should I service my HVAC system in Colorado Springs?

We recommend two tune-ups per year: one in spring (April–May) before the cooling season, and one in fall (September–October) before heating season. Colorado's dramatic climate swings — from summer heat waves to winter blizzards — put extra stress on HVAC equipment compared to milder climates. Bi-annual maintenance catches issues early and keeps your system running efficiently at altitude, where systems work harder than at sea level.

Does Colorado's high altitude affect my HVAC system's performance?

Yes, absolutely. Colorado Springs sits at roughly 6,000+ feet elevation, which means thinner air with less oxygen. This affects combustion in gas furnaces — they need to be properly de-rated for altitude so they burn cleanly and safely. Air conditioners and heat pumps also work harder in thinner air. Our technicians are trained specifically for Colorado's altitude conditions and ensure all equipment is properly calibrated for optimal performance and safety at elevation.

What's the best heating system for Colorado's cold winters?

For Colorado Springs, a high-efficiency gas furnace (96%+ AFUE) is often the best primary heat source — it handles the sub-zero nights that occasionally hit the Pikes Peak region. For mild winter days, a dual-fuel heat pump system is increasingly popular: the heat pump handles temperatures above about 30°F efficiently, and the gas furnace kicks in for bitter cold snaps. We can assess your home, budget, and usage to recommend the ideal system. Heat pumps have also improved dramatically for cold climates and are worth considering for the right home.

How do I know if I need AC repair or a full AC replacement?

A useful rule of thumb: if repair costs exceed 50% of the replacement cost, or your unit is over 10–15 years old and breaking down repeatedly, replacement is usually the better financial choice. Signs you need replacement: R-22 refrigerant (now expensive and discontinued), SEER rating below 14, frequent repairs in the last 2 years, uneven cooling despite repairs. We always give you an honest assessment — we'll tell you when a simple repair will buy you several more years, and we'll tell you when you're throwing money at a failing system.

Why is my Colorado home so dry in winter, and can HVAC help?

Colorado is one of the driest states in the country, and winter air holds very little moisture. When you heat that dry outdoor air inside, the relative humidity can drop to 15–20% — far below the comfortable 35–45% range. This causes chapped lips, dry skin, static electricity, wood furniture cracking, and makes you feel colder even at the same temperature. The solution is a whole-home humidifier integrated into your HVAC system. These units are far more effective than portable humidifiers and maintain ideal humidity automatically. Many of our customers say it's the single biggest improvement to their winter home comfort.
